How to Ride a Matatu Like a Local

Published on June 22, 2024 by Yusuf

There is no experience more authentically Nairobi than a ride on a matatu. These vibrant, privately-owned minibuses are the lifeblood of the city's transport system, famous for their blaring music, colorful graffiti art, and sometimes-chaotic energy. For a first-time visitor, it can seem intimidating, but with a few tips, you'll be navigating the city like a pro and getting a real taste of local life.

1. Know Your Route and Number

Every matatu follows a specific numbered route. Before you hop on, you need to know the number for your destination. Ask a local, your host, or a guide for the correct number. The route number is usually painted prominently on the front and side of the matatu. Don't be afraid to shout your destination to the "makanga" (the conductor) hanging out the door; they'll tell you if it's the right one.

2. Have Your Fare Ready

Matatu fares are cheap, but they often change depending on the time of day, traffic, and even the weather. Fares can range from Ksh 30 to Ksh 150 (about $0.25 to $1.20 USD). It's best to have small bills or coins ready. You pay the makanga directly. Watch what others are paying to get a sense of the correct fare for your journey.

3. Embrace the Squeeze

Matatus are all about efficiency, which means they fit as many people as possible. Don't be surprised if you're sharing a row meant for three with four or even five other people. It's all part of the experience! Just be mindful of your belongings and keep your wallet and phone in a secure pocket.

4. Signal Your Stop

There are no official "next stop" announcements. You need to tell the makanga when you want to get off. The common way to do this is to say "Shukisha!" (meaning "drop me") or "Hapo kando!" ("on the side here"). You can also tap a coin against the metal window frame, a universal signal that you want to alight at the next stop.
